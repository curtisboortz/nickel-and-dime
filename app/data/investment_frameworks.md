# Investment Frameworks Knowledge Base

Use these frameworks to ground your portfolio analysis in established investment
thinking. Reference specific principles by name when they apply to the user's
situation. Combine frameworks when multiple perspectives are relevant.

---

## Benjamin Graham — The Intelligent Investor

**Core thesis:** The market is a voting machine in the short run and a weighing
machine in the long run. Investors protect themselves by buying with a margin of
safety — paying meaningfully less than a conservative estimate of intrinsic value.

**Principles:**
- **Mr. Market allegory:** The market is an emotional counterparty who offers you
  prices every day. You are free to accept or ignore. Use Mr. Market's mood swings
  to your advantage; never let them dictate your decisions.
- **Margin of safety:** The single most important concept in investing. The gap
  between price and a conservative intrinsic-value estimate is the investor's
  buffer against error. The wider the margin, the lower the risk.
- **Defensive vs. enterprising investor:** A defensive investor diversifies broadly,
  buys quality, avoids overpaying (P/E < 15, P/B < 1.5, consistent dividend
  history, manageable debt). An enterprising investor does deeper research and
  accepts concentrated bets, but still demands a margin of safety.
- **Stock selection criteria (defensive):** At least 10 years of positive earnings,
  uninterrupted dividends for 20+ years, moderate P/E (< 15 on 3-year average
  earnings), moderate P/B (< 1.5), and a current ratio > 2.
- **Distinguish investment from speculation:** An investment operation is one which,
  upon thorough analysis, promises safety of principal and an adequate return.
  Operations not meeting these requirements are speculative.

**Key quotes:**
- "The investor's chief problem — and even his worst enemy — is likely to be himself."
- "In the short run, the market is a voting machine but in the long run, it is a
  weighing machine."
- "The margin of safety is always dependent on the price paid."

**When to apply:** Evaluating individual stock positions, assessing whether the
portfolio is investing or speculating, discussing valuation discipline, flagging
positions bought at high P/E multiples.

## Warren Buffett — Berkshire Hathaway Letters & Practice

**Core thesis:** Buy wonderful businesses at fair prices with durable competitive
advantages, and hold them for decades. Concentrate capital in your highest-
conviction ideas within your circle of competence.

**Principles:**
- **Circle of competence:** Only invest in businesses you genuinely understand. The
  size of the circle matters less than knowing its boundaries. When the user holds
  complex or opaque positions, flag the competence question.
- **Economic moats:** Durable competitive advantages come in four forms — brand power
  (Coca-Cola), high switching costs (enterprise software), network effects
  (payment rails), and cost advantages (scale economics). Prefer businesses with
  widening moats.
- **Owner earnings:** Reported earnings are an accounting artifact. What matters is
  net income + depreciation/amortization − required capex. This is the true cash
  flow available to the owner.
- **"Wonderful company at a fair price > fair company at a wonderful price":**
  Quality compounds. A business that earns high returns on capital and can reinvest
  at those same rates is far more valuable over time than a cheap stock with
  mediocre economics.
- **Temperament over intellect:** Investing success correlates more with emotional
  discipline than IQ. The ability to sit through drawdowns without panic-selling is
  the primary edge for most investors.
- **Concentrated portfolios:** Diversification is protection against ignorance.
  If you understand what you own, 10-15 positions can deliver better risk-adjusted
  returns than 100 positions. But only if the analysis is sound.
- **Time arbitrage:** The stock market transfers wealth from the impatient to the
  patient. When the user has a long time horizon, emphasize compounding.

**Key quotes:**
- "Rule No. 1: Never lose money. Rule No. 2: Never forget Rule No. 1."
- "Be fearful when others are greedy, and greedy when others are fearful."
- "Our favorite holding period is forever."
- "Price is what you pay. Value is what you get."

**When to apply:** Evaluating position concentration, discussing hold vs. sell
decisions, analyzing business quality of individual holdings, long-term compounding
discussions, situations where the user is tempted to trade frequently.

## Charlie Munger — Mental Models & Rational Thinking

**Core thesis:** Worldly wisdom comes from mastering multiple mental models across
disciplines. In investing, the key is to avoid stupidity rather than seek
brilliance — invert, always invert.

**Principles:**
- **Inversion:** Instead of asking "how do I succeed?", ask "what would guarantee
  failure, and how do I avoid that?" Applied to portfolios: what would cause
  permanent capital loss, and is the portfolio protected against those scenarios?
- **Second-order thinking:** First-order thinking says "this company is great, buy
  it." Second-order thinking asks "everyone knows it's great, so is that already
  priced in?" Always push to the next level of consequence.
- **Lollapalooza effects:** When multiple psychological biases align in the same
  direction, the result is extreme and often irrational. Market bubbles and panics
  are lollapalooza effects. Recognize when several biases are compounding.
- **Quality over cheapness:** "A great business at a fair price is superior to a
  fair business at a great price." Over time, a stock tends to converge with the
  economics of the underlying business. Cheap and deteriorating stays cheap.
- **Patience as edge:** "The big money is not in the buying and the selling, but in
  the waiting." Most investors trade too much. Sitting still with quality holdings
  is an underrated strategy.
- **Avoid deprival super-reaction:** People feel losses roughly 2x as intensely as
  equivalent gains (loss aversion). This causes panic selling at bottoms. When
  markets drop sharply, remind the user of this cognitive bias.
- **Checklist discipline:** Before any major portfolio change, run through a mental
  checklist: Am I within my competence? Is there a margin of safety? What's the
  downside? Am I being influenced by recent events (recency bias)?

**Key quotes:**
- "All I want to know is where I'm going to die, so I'll never go there."
- "It is remarkable how much long-term advantage people like us have gotten by
  trying to be consistently not stupid, instead of trying to be very intelligent."
- "Invert, always invert."

**When to apply:** When the user is considering a drastic portfolio change, during
market sell-offs (loss aversion check), when evaluating whether "cheap" stocks are
truly cheap or value traps, when multiple bullish or bearish arguments stack up
suspiciously.

## Ray Dalio — Principles, All Weather & the Economic Machine

**Core thesis:** The economy is a machine driven by credit cycles and human nature.
True diversification means balancing risk across four economic environments, not
just diversifying across stocks. The "Holy Grail" is 15+ uncorrelated return
streams.

**Principles:**
- **Four economic quadrants:** Every economic period is a combination of growth
  and inflation: (1) rising growth + rising inflation, (2) rising growth + falling
  inflation, (3) falling growth + rising inflation (stagflation), (4) falling
  growth + falling inflation (deflation). A robust portfolio has assets that
  perform in each quadrant.
- **All Weather target allocation:** The classic All Weather portfolio: ~30%
  stocks, ~40% long-term bonds, ~15% intermediate bonds, ~7.5% gold, ~7.5%
  commodities. The goal is equal risk contribution from each quadrant, not equal
  dollar allocation.
- **Risk parity:** Weight positions by inverse volatility so each asset contributes
  roughly equal risk. Equities are 3-4x more volatile than bonds, so an
  equal-dollar split (50/50) is actually ~90/10 in risk terms.
- **Holy Grail of Investing:** With 15+ truly uncorrelated return streams, you can
  reduce portfolio risk by ~80% without reducing expected return. Each additional
  uncorrelated stream has diminishing marginal benefit after ~15.
- **Credit cycles:** Short-term debt cycles (~5-8 years) and long-term debt cycles
  (~75-100 years) drive most economic fluctuations. Understanding where you are in
  the cycle informs positioning: early cycle favors risk assets, late cycle favors
  havens.
- **Radical open-mindedness:** The biggest investing mistakes come from blind spots.
  Stress-test every thesis. If you can't articulate the strongest argument against
  your position, you don't understand it well enough.

**Key quotes:**
- "He who lives by the crystal ball will eat shattered glass."
- "Diversifying well is the most important thing you need to do in order to invest
  well."
- "The biggest mistake investors make is to believe that what happened in the
  recent past is likely to persist."

**When to apply:** Assessing overall portfolio balance across economic environments,
when portfolio is >70% equities (growth-surprise concentration), discussing
bonds/gold/commodities allocation, analyzing macro positioning relative to the
current economic cycle, evaluating the user's FRED data and yield curve signals.

## Howard Marks — The Most Important Thing & Market Cycles

**Core thesis:** Superior investing is not about buying good assets but about
buying assets at good prices. Risk is the probability of permanent capital loss,
not volatility. Markets move in pendulum swings between euphoria and despair.

**Principles:**
- **Second-level thinking (defined):** First-level: "It's a good company, let's
  buy." Second-level: "It's a good company, everyone thinks it's great, and it's
  overpriced — let's sell." Superior results require thinking differently AND
  better than the consensus.
- **The pendulum:** Investor psychology swings between greed and fear, between
  euphoria and panic. The pendulum spends very little time at the midpoint. The
  best opportunities arise at the extremes. Use the Fear & Greed index as a
  proxy.
- **Risk as permanent loss:** Risk is not volatility (standard deviation). Risk is
  the probability and magnitude of permanent capital loss. A volatile asset held
  at the right price is less risky than a stable asset bought at a bubble price.
- **The relationship between price and risk:** High prices mean high risk, low
  prices mean low risk. This is the opposite of what most people feel (recent
  gains make people feel safe, recent losses make them feel afraid).
- **"Being right for the right reasons":** A correct outcome from flawed reasoning
  teaches nothing and builds bad habits. Process matters more than any single
  outcome.
- **Knowing what you don't know:** The future is uncertain. Express conviction in
  ranges, not point estimates. Avoid false precision. Acknowledge the limits of
  any forecast.
- **Contrarian positioning:** Consensus thinking delivers consensus returns. Above-
  average results require non-consensus positioning that turns out to be correct.
  But being contrarian for its own sake is just as dangerous.

**Key quotes:**
- "The most important thing is not what you buy, but what you pay for it."
- "There are old investors, and there are bold investors, but there are no old,
  bold investors."
- "Experience is what you got when you didn't get what you wanted."

**When to apply:** When sentiment indicators are at extremes (F&G >80 or <20),
evaluating whether positions were bought at fair prices, discussing risk in terms
the user can act on (loss probability vs. volatility), rebalancing into beaten-down
asset classes.

## Bill Ackman — Concentrated Value & Activist Investing

**Core thesis:** Concentrate capital in a small number of high-conviction positions
in simple, predictable businesses. Active engagement with management creates
catalysts that unlock value and serve as an additional margin of safety.

**Principles:**
- **Simple, predictable businesses:** Invest in companies with straightforward
  business models, recurring revenue, and predictable free cash flow. If you can't
  explain the business in two sentences, it's too complex.
- **Concentrated portfolios (8-12 positions):** Deep research on a few names beats
  shallow research on many. Concentration magnifies both returns and losses, so
  conviction must be anchored in thorough analysis.
- **Management quality matters:** Great businesses with bad management underperform.
  Look for competent, honest, owner-oriented management teams. Capital allocation
  skill is the single most important CEO trait.
- **Catalysts as margin of safety:** An identified event that will unlock value
  (spin-off, cost restructuring, management change, share buyback) provides a
  second form of margin of safety beyond price discount alone.
- **Downside-first thinking:** Spend more time analyzing what could go wrong than
  what could go right. If the downside is limited and the upside is significant,
  the asymmetry is favorable.
- **Long-term orientation despite activism:** Even in activist situations, the
  holding period is typically 3-5 years. Impatience is the enemy of compounding.

**Key quotes:**
- "I look for simple, predictable, free-cash-flow-generative businesses with
  a dominant market position."
- "The best investments are the ones where you have a lot more to gain than you
  have to lose."

**When to apply:** When evaluating concentrated vs. diversified approaches, analyzing
individual stock positions for business quality, discussing position sizing for
high-conviction ideas, evaluating management quality of the user's holdings.

## Peter Lynch — One Up On Wall Street

**Core thesis:** Individual investors have structural advantages over institutions:
they can invest in what they know from daily life, hold through volatility without
career risk, and own small companies institutions can't touch.

**Principles:**
- **Invest in what you know:** Your job, hobbies, and shopping habits give you an
  information edge. If you notice a product or store gaining popularity before Wall
  Street does, that's a legitimate research insight. But "know" means doing the
  financial homework, not just liking the product.
- **PEG ratio:** A stock's P/E ratio divided by its earnings growth rate. PEG < 1
  suggests undervaluation; PEG > 2 suggests overvaluation. Simple but surprisingly
  effective as a first-pass screen.
- **Six stock categories:** (1) Slow growers — mature, dividend-paying, low growth.
  (2) Stalwarts — large, steady 10-12% earnings growth. (3) Fast growers — small,
  aggressive, 20-25%+ growth. (4) Cyclicals — earnings tied to economic cycles.
  (5) Turnarounds — distressed companies recovering. (6) Asset plays — hidden
  assets on the balance sheet worth more than the stock price.
- **The cocktail party indicator:** When strangers at parties want stock tips from
  you, the market is near a top. When nobody wants to talk about stocks, it's
  closer to a bottom. A humorous but directionally useful sentiment gauge.
- **Know what you own and why you own it:** If you can't explain in two minutes why
  you own a stock, you shouldn't own it. Apply this test to every position.
- **The 10-bagger mindset:** Don't sell winners too early. The biggest portfolio
  returns come from a few positions that multiply many times over. Trimming winners
  and holding losers ("pulling the flowers and watering the weeds") is the most
  common retail investor mistake.
- **Earnings, earnings, earnings:** In the long run, stock prices follow earnings.
  Revenue growth, margin trends, and free cash flow trajectory matter more than
  any technical indicator or macro forecast.

**Key quotes:**
- "Know what you own, and know why you own it."
- "The person who turns over the most rocks wins the game."
- "In the long run, it's not just how much money you make that matters — it's how
  much you keep after the effects of inflation and taxes."
- "Go for a business that any idiot can run — because sooner or later, any idiot
  is going to run it."

**When to apply:** When discussing individual stock picks vs. index funds, evaluating
growth stocks (PEG), discussing holding periods and the temptation to sell winners,
categorizing the user's holdings by Lynch's six categories, addressing retail
investor behavioral mistakes.

## John Bogle — The Little Book of Common Sense Investing

**Core thesis:** Costs are the single most reliable predictor of future fund
returns. The average investor captures the market return minus costs, so minimizing
costs through broad index funds is the mathematically optimal strategy for most
people.

**Principles:**
- **Cost drag compounds destructively:** A 1% annual fee on a $100,000 portfolio
  over 30 years at 8% growth costs ~$230,000 in lost terminal wealth. Always flag
  holdings with expense ratios above 0.20% and positions with high turnover.
- **Reversion to the mean:** Funds that outperform in one period tend to
  underperform in the next, and vice versa. Past performance is a poor predictor
  of future results. Chasing hot funds is statistically a losing strategy.
- **Investment return vs. speculative return:** Total stock market returns =
  dividend yield + earnings growth (investment return) + change in P/E multiple
  (speculative return). Over long periods, speculative return averages to zero.
  Investment return is what's real and sustainable.
- **Stay the course:** The biggest risk for most investors is behavioral — selling
  in panic during drawdowns or chasing performance in rallies. Market timing
  destroys more wealth than market declines. Time in the market beats timing the
  market.
- **Three-fund portfolio:** Total US stock market index + total international stock
  index + total bond market index. This covers virtually all investable assets at
  minimal cost. Complexity beyond this requires justification.
- **Don't look for the needle; buy the haystack:** Instead of trying to pick
  winning stocks or time markets, own the entire market. The haystack always
  contains the needle, and you never miss a winner.
- **Simplicity wins:** Every layer of complexity (active management, alternative
  strategies, leverage, frequent trading) adds cost and usually subtracts return.
  The burden of proof is on complexity, not simplicity.

**Key quotes:**
- "Don't look for the needle in the haystack. Just buy the haystack."
- "In investing, you get what you don't pay for."
- "Time is your friend; impulse is your enemy."
- "The stock market is a giant distraction from the business of investing."

**When to apply:** Evaluating the user's overall cost structure (expense ratios,
trading frequency), comparing active vs. passive strategies, discussing portfolio
simplification, addressing the urge to trade frequently, long-term wealth
accumulation planning.
